Overview

During the COVID-19 pandemic, visualizations became one of the most important tools for understanding the world. From dashboards tracking cases to animated charts in the New York Times, these graphics became part of our daily lives—sometimes shaping behavior as much as the underlying data itself. This project explores how visual storytelling evolved under crisis conditions, and how that evolution impacted how we viewed the pandemic. We analyze 173 data-driven articles from the “What’s Going On in This Graph?” series published between 2015 and 2024. Our goal was to explore how the urgency, complexity, and volume of pandemic data reshaped visual design practices—both in terms of graphic form (e.g., interactivity, color use, complexity) and thematic focus (e.g., health and risk communication). We also wished to explore how these changes under pressure may have affected how we viewed the pandemic. By combining historical analysis, literature review, and our own empirical study, we provide a timeline of this transformation and reflect on its broader implications.
